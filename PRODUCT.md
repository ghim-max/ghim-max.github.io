# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Hiring managers and recruiters at tech, fintech, and product companies who scan many portfolios. They decide quickly — within 30 seconds — whether a candidate is worth their time. They're looking for signal: can this person communicate? Do they have a point of view? Is there credible work behind the surface? They are busy and skeptical of polish that substitutes for substance.

## Product Purpose

A personal portfolio that earns trust and demonstrates PM caliber fast. The site exists to get interviews. It succeeds when a hiring manager finishes the homepage and immediately wants to know more — not because of visual flair, but because the work and the person behind it feel real, credible, and distinct from every other portfolio in their inbox.

## Brand Personality

Bold · Grounded · Purposeful.

The narrative behind this: a non-linear path taken without a safety net — architecture to corporate development to fintech product — each leap deliberate, each landing built on the one before. Work aimed at real-world impact: embedded insurance for underserved communities in Zambia and Nigeria, digital payment wallets at national scale in DRC and Ghana, remittance tools for dormitory workers. Stays humble. Doesn't perform.

Voice is confident and direct without being loud. There is nothing here for decoration's sake.

## Anti-references

- **Generic PM portfolio** — Cream/beige background, neat case study card grid, template structure. Reads as everyone else. No voice, no position.
- **Tech-bro minimalism** — Dark mode, monospace everywhere, GitHub-adjacent palette. Signals developer, not product thinker. Wrong register.
- **Agency-designer style** — Flashy animations, bold color blocks, overstyled layouts. Design becomes the product instead of serving it. Too loud for this brief.
- **LinkedIn-in-HTML** — Pure CV structure, bullet-point history, no narrative, no designed experience. Doesn't demonstrate the ability to communicate.

## Design Principles

1. **The work is the credential, not the wrapper.** Design should direct attention to case studies, not compete with them. Any element that makes you notice the design is a failure.
2. **Specificity over categories.** "Embedded insurance for underserved communities in Zambia and Nigeria" beats "product management" every time. Structure and copy should surface precision, not bury it in generalities.
3. **A non-linear path is a differentiator.** Architecture → corporate development → fintech PM is not a liability; it is a story worth telling legibly. The site should make the arc clear without reducing it to a timeline.
4. **Restraint is credibility.** Every visual choice should feel made for a reason. Excess undermines "grounded." If a design element doesn't serve the content, it goes.
5. **Conviction, not performance.** Bold means taking a position, not making noise. The design should feel like someone who knows what they're doing without needing to prove it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum throughout. Standard web accommodations: reduced motion alternatives for any animation, sufficient color contrast for all text (≥4.5:1 body, ≥3:1 large text), keyboard navigation for all interactive elements, skip navigation link.
